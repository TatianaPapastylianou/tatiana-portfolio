selected work

case one

foundation

enterprise software platform, logistics and compliance lead product designer nine months

the problem

Five product surfaces had drifted apart over six years. Forty-seven button variants across the platform. Two design teams, no shared governance, and a component library that nobody trusted.

Design debt was showing up as engineering debt. Every new feature needed a custom control.

the approach

We did not build a library upfront. We shipped tokens and four components, measured which teams picked them up, and expanded only what was used.

Governance stayed with a small working team rather than a review committee. One designer and one engineer from each product held write access. Office hours on Thursdays. Everything else was asynchronous.

the outcome

  • ninety-two percent component adoption across three quarters.
  • thirty-eight percent faster design-to-development handoff.
  • one thousand two hundred hours of engineering time saved in year one.

one decision We refused to document anything that had fewer than three uses in production. The library stayed small on purpose. Teams stopped treating it as a proposal and started treating it as a floor.

case two

tread

consumer language-learning product, several million monthly learners lead accessibility designer eleven months

the problem

Core exercises relied on drag-and-drop. Learners with motor impairments were abandoning at the fill-in-the-blank stage. Support tickets repeated the same sentence for a year.

A formal audit found thirty-one WCAG two point one AA violations across the primary learning surface, most of them on the exercises that carried the most traffic.

the approach

We redesigned the drag-and-drop pattern as click-to-place. Tap once to pick up a token. Tap again to place it. The motion nobody asked for was simply removed.

The alternative ran alongside the original for six weeks with a matched cohort. Then we rebuilt contrast, focus order, and screen reader labels from scratch.

the outcome

  • forty-one to seventy-eight percent exercise completion rate for learners using assistive technology.
  • fourteen percent higher overall exercise completion across all users.
  • zero WCAG two point one AA violations at the final audit.

one decision Click-to-place became the default, not the accessibility alternative. It tested faster for everyone. Drag stayed available, but it stopped being the primary path. The right pattern for the constrained user turned out to be the right pattern for the whole product.

case three

counter

european luxury retailer, direct-to-consumer design lead eight months

the problem

Customer service chat handled a growing share of pre-purchase questions. The obvious answer was to hire more agents. The real answer was not.

A pure language model could write confident responses when it was wrong, which is worse than silence in luxury retail. Brand voice drifted by the second paragraph.

the approach

We built a system where the model proposed responses and a human agent approved, edited, or rejected them before anything reached the customer. The approval interface became the central design problem.

Agents needed to work at the speed of chat, not the speed of editing. The model's confidence became a visual weight rather than a number. Agents read it within two days. A twelve-week multi-armed bandit test ran across four response styles.

the outcome

  • two hundred and twenty percent higher agent throughput.
  • nineteen percent conversion lift on chat-initiated sessions.
  • eleven seconds average response time, from two minutes and forty seconds.

one decision Rejected responses routed back as training signal without adding review load for the agent. Quality improved in the background while the team kept its attention on the customer. The approval gate was the feature, not the bottleneck.

principles

what the work rests on.

  1. one The interface is the product to the person using it.
  2. two Restraint scales. Features do not.
  3. three Accessibility is the baseline, not a phase of the project.
  4. four Measure what ships. Then ship less of it.
