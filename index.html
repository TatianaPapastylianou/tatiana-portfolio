1.

case study

foundation

enterprise software platform, logistics and compliance lead product designer 9 months

the problem

5 product surfaces had drifted apart over 6 years. 47 button variants across the platform. 2 design teams, no shared governance, and a component library that nobody trusted.

Design debt was showing up as engineering debt. Every new feature needed a custom control.

the approach

We did not build a library upfront. We shipped tokens and 4 components, measured which teams picked them up, and expanded only what was used.

Governance stayed with a small working team rather than a review committee. 1 designer and 1 engineer from each product held write access. Office hours on Thursdays. Everything else was asynchronous.

the outcome

  • 92% component adoption across 3 quarters.
  • 38% faster design-to-development handoff.
  • 1,200 hours of engineering time saved in year 1.
1 decision

We refused to document anything that had fewer than 3 uses in production. The library stayed small on purpose. Teams stopped treating it as a proposal and started treating it as a floor.

2.

case study

tread

consumer language-learning product, several million monthly learners lead accessibility designer 11 months

the problem

Core exercises relied on drag-and-drop. Learners with motor impairments were abandoning at the fill-in-the-blank stage. Support tickets repeated the same sentence for a year.

A formal audit found 31 WCAG 2.1 AA violations across the primary learning surface, most of them on the exercises that carried the most traffic.

the approach

We redesigned the drag-and-drop pattern as click-to-place. Tap once to pick up a token. Tap again to place it. The motion nobody asked for was simply removed.

The alternative ran alongside the original for 6 weeks with a matched cohort. Then we rebuilt contrast, focus order, and screen reader labels from scratch.

the outcome

  • 41% to 78% exercise completion for learners using assistive technology.
  • 14% higher overall exercise completion across all users.
  • 0 WCAG 2.1 AA violations at the final audit.
1 decision

Click-to-place became the default, not the accessibility alternative. It tested faster for everyone. Drag stayed available, but it stopped being the primary path. The right pattern for the constrained user turned out to be the right pattern for the whole product.

3.

case study

counter

european luxury retailer, direct-to-consumer design lead 8 months

the problem

Customer service chat handled a growing share of pre-purchase questions. The obvious answer was to hire more agents. The real answer was not.

A pure language model could write confident responses when it was wrong, which is worse than silence in luxury retail. Brand voice drifted by the 2nd paragraph.

the approach

We built a system where the model proposed responses and a human agent approved, edited, or rejected them before anything reached the customer. The approval interface became the central design problem.

Agents needed to work at the speed of chat, not the speed of editing. The model's confidence became a visual weight rather than a number. Agents read it within 2 days. A 12-week multi-armed bandit test ran across 4 response styles.

the outcome

  • 220% higher agent throughput.
  • 19% conversion lift on chat-initiated sessions.
  • 11 seconds average response time, from 2 minutes 40 seconds.
1 decision

Rejected responses routed back as training signal without adding review load for the agent. Quality improved in the background while the team kept its attention on the customer. The approval gate was the feature, not the bottleneck.
