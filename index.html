1.

case study

foundation

enterprise software platform, logistics and compliance lead product designer 9 months

the problem

5 product surfaces had drifted apart over 6 years. 47 button variants across the platform. 2 design teams, no shared governance, and a component library that nobody trusted.

Design debt was showing up as engineering debt. Every new feature needed a custom control.

the approach

We did not build a library upfront. We shipped tokens and 4 components, measured which teams picked them up, and expanded only what was used.

Governance stayed with a small working team rather than a review committee. 1 designer and 1 engineer from each product held write access. Office hours on Thursdays. Everything else was asynchronous.

the outcome

  • 92% component adoption across 3 quarters.
  • 38% faster design-to-development handoff.
  • 1,200 hours of engineering time saved in year 1.
1 decision

We refused to document anything that had fewer than 3 uses in production. The library stayed small on purpose. Teams started treating it as a floor instead of arguing about it.

2.

case study

tread

consumer language-learning product, several million monthly learners lead accessibility designer 11 months

the problem

Core exercises relied on drag-and-drop. Learners with motor impairments were abandoning at the fill-in-the-blank stage. Support tickets repeated the same sentence for a year.

A formal audit found 31 WCAG 2.1 AA violations across the primary learning surface, most of them on the exercises that carried the most traffic.

the approach

We redesigned drag-and-drop as click-to-place. Tap to pick up a token. Tap to place it. We took the motion out entirely.

The new pattern ran alongside the original for 6 weeks with a matched cohort. Then we rebuilt contrast, focus order, and screen reader labels from scratch.

the outcome

  • 41% to 78% exercise completion for learners using assistive technology.
  • 14% higher overall exercise completion across all users.
  • 0 WCAG 2.1 AA violations at the final audit.
1 decision

Click-to-place became the default. Drag stayed available as an option. What we built for the constrained user turned out to be faster for everyone.

3.

case study

counter

european luxury retailer, direct-to-consumer design lead 8 months

the problem

Customer service chat was handling a growing share of pre-purchase questions. Hiring more agents was the obvious response. It would also have been the wrong one.

A language model on its own wrote confident responses when it was wrong, which is worse than silence in luxury retail. Brand voice drifted by the 2nd paragraph.

the approach

We built a system where the model proposed a response and a human agent approved, edited, or rejected it before the customer saw anything. The approval interface was where most of the design work went.

Agents were working at chat speed, not editing speed. The model's confidence had to be readable at a glance, so we turned the number into a visual weight. Agents were reading it accurately within 2 days. A 12-week multi-armed bandit test ran across 4 response styles.

the outcome

  • 220% higher agent throughput.
  • 19% conversion lift on chat-initiated sessions.
  • 11 seconds average response time, from 2 minutes 40 seconds.
1 decision

Rejected responses fed back into the model as training signal, so the agent never had to do extra work for it. Quality kept improving in the background, and the team kept its attention on the customer in front of it.

4.

case study

estate

european real-estate operator, 10 internal applications senior product designer 2 years

the problem

10 separate applications covering the property lifecycle: acquisition, maintenance, reconstruction, leasing, tenant management, disposition. Each had grown its own UI. Operators were moving between 4 of them to finish a single tenant onboarding.

The same address was re-entered in 3 places before a lease got signed. Training a new property manager took 6 weeks.

the approach

I mapped the operator's week across all 10 apps. The same 18 tasks kept surfacing under different labels. We built those as a shared pattern library and rebuilt the apps around it.

Where 2 apps were doing the same job under different departmental labels, we collapsed them. The remaining surfaces kept their distinct character where the work itself was distinct.

the outcome

  • 10 to 7 applications after consolidation.
  • 44% faster tenant onboarding.
  • 62% of screens covered by the shared pattern library.
1 decision

We stopped treating each app as its own product. 18 shared patterns carried most of every screen. The remaining work is where each app earns its name.

5.

case study

chorus

consumer language-learning product, web, iOS, Android cross-platform design lead ongoing

the problem

One content model, three platforms, three design instincts. The same exercise felt different on iOS, Android, and web. Learners switching devices lost momentum because a fill-in-the-blank behaved differently each morning.

The content team was authoring once and hoping for consistency. It was not arriving.

the approach

I designed the exercise as an interaction contract — what the learner needs to do, what counts as correct, how a mistake feels — and then three platform expressions of it. Typography, motion, and touch targets shifted to match platform conventions. The decision the learner had to make stayed identical.

24 shared exercise types got the same treatment. The content model stayed single-source.

the outcome

  • 92% cross-device retention at 30 days.
  • 3 platforms sharing 1 content pipeline.
  • 24 exercise types shipped across all three runtimes.
1 decision

We did not unify the UI. We unified the decision the learner had to make. Each platform keeps its own accent, as long as what the learner is deciding looks the same.

6.

case study

proof

consumer math-learning product, web, iOS, Android senior product designer 9 months

the problem

Mathematics is harder to type than to write. The first learners who hit equation input on a phone keyboard lost 40% of their cohort inside the first minute. The same learners stayed with paper worksheets for 20 minutes and did not complain.

Typing was the friction, not the math.

the approach

I prototyped 4 input methods — stylus, keyboard with a math overlay, tap-to-build, and speech — and tested each with actual learners across 4 grade bands. Tap-to-build shipped as the default. The keyboard stayed available for learners who wanted it.

Tap-to-build became the primitive. Every new exercise type since has been designed against it.

the outcome

  • 34s to 11s time from question to first answer.
  • 63% of learners finish a lesson in one sitting.
  • 0 complaints about typing math in 90 days after launch.
1 decision

We stopped trying to match the keyboard. Math on a touch screen deserves a touch interaction. Tap-to-build is the primitive the rest of the app was rewritten against.

7.

case study

forge

internal authoring tool for two learner-facing products design lead 14 months

the problem

Content editors were building lessons in a generic CMS and writing around the tool, not inside it. 40 minutes to author an exercise that rendered in the app for 30 seconds. The edits that mattered most — small copy changes, token swaps — felt the furthest from the learner.

the approach

I designed Generator as an editor that mirrors the learner surface 1-to-1. Editors see what the learner will see while they write. Metadata sits in a parallel panel. Preview is not a separate mode; it is the writing surface.

Structured fields protect the content model. Everything else is direct manipulation on the rendered lesson.

the outcome

  • 11 minutes average time to author an exercise, from 40.
  • 3x lesson output per editor per week.
  • 58% to 91% first-time-right rate on published exercises.
1 decision

The preview stopped being a button and became the canvas. Editors never leave the learner's view. What feels right for the editor is what the learner gets.
